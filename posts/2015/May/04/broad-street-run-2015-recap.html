Broad Street Run 2015 Recap

in news  

2015 gave me the opportunity to run my 4th Broad Street Run in Philadelphia, PA. This year was different, as I was part of a growing team that was more than just a group of runners. We were “framily”. Each and every one of us were there from the beginning, through training, and at the finish line, supporting each other through ups and downs.

This is my journey down Broad Street!

November 2014

My buddy Andy and I decided to get a team together to run Broad Street and raise funds for the American Cancer Society. This was the third year in a row that we both ran as fundraisers for DetermiNation, and the second in a row where we were teammates. We decided we needed a better name than our “mildly offensive” name from last year. So, taking a cue from some of our hobbies and interests, we came up with the “Low And Slow Team (LAST)”.

[Background on the team. Andy started with 3 members back in 2013. In 2014, we had 16 runners from around the United States. This year, we had 52 runners from around the country. The growth in members was fantastic, not only for the comaraderie, but for raising even more funds for cancer awareness and research.]

I was jonesed for this race this year. I had made major strides in my running over the past 6 months. My pace had improved, I had lost some weight, my technique was getting better. I set a goal for myself that I could set a personal record (PR) for myself this year of 1:35:00 (9:30/mile), with a stretch goal of 1:30:00 (9:00/mile). I knew I could do this with the right training.

January 2015

Fundraising was going well. I had a big turnout from a block pool for a major sporting event (they don’t like when you use the term for the game without licensing ). I put out a challenge goal to my friends and family that involved cutting my growing hair. Needless to say, I exceeded that goal well before the two months were up!

February 2015

I was pumped. I dropped my New Year’s weight and was ready to go full-force on training. Then it happened. Bad weather, snow and ice. I slipped while putting stuff in my car before going to work, landed on my back. With chiropractic therapy and rest, I was now out of training for 6 weeks.

April 2015

Beginning of the month, I’ve been given the go-ahead to start training again. I start right after cutting my hair, then it happens again - another setback. This time, I’ve gotten a bad cold and cough that, as of the writing of this article, has been with me for about 6 weeks. I still trained a bit early on, but my lungs never liked me. I struggled, still making some good times, getting a few good training runs in with friends and solo, but I knew at this point that my PR goal was not going to be met.

I saw my doctor two weeks before BSR, was put on antibiotics and more allergy meds. I thought I felt fine, even though I was undertrained. I knew I could run BSR, and nothing was stopping me…

May 2015

The time was here. I had been training and fundraising with this awesome group of people for months, many of whom I had never met in person. The big get-together was only days away.

In just six months, this rag-tag group of runners from around the country had raised almost $31k for the American Cancer Society, and now we were going to complete this first journey together, come hell or high-water!

May 2nd - The dinner the night before

While many of my teammates had been in town for a few days by this point, getting to meet one another, this was probably the first real get-together of the majority of us - at the DetermiNation pre-race dinner.

Followed by prep…

May 3rd - Race Day

Getting up at the crack of pre-dawn after a later night than planned wasn’t easy. I was to pick up a teammate of mine and drive down to Citizens Bank Park with her, but she unfortunately had ankle issues and decided not to run that morning. Smart move, in my opinion. If only I had been that smart with my lungs, considering the high pollen count predicted for the day.

After taking the subway up to the starting line, where the DNation tent was (yay, private port-a-potties!), it was now a waiting game for each corral to line up to race. So, what to do but hang out with good friends, have fun, and put tattoos on each other!

Race Time!

I knew this wasn’t going to be my ultimate PR race, but I felt as though I was still going to get a PR. I started off nice and easy, feeling good and skipping my first 2 walk intervals. This felt good, but I wasn’t at maximum.

At around 3 miles, the heat started to hit me (probably only about 60, but very warm for me). I had to take it easy and ease off a bit. That, and the allergies were starting to kick in. I felt my lungs starting to strain a bit, and I was not going to exit the course. I was finishing no matter what.

After I rounded City Hall, I knew this wasn’t my day. My lungs were burning, but my feet and legs felt fine. I kept going with my intervals, maybe taking a few extra walks than planned, but I kept on chugging until mile 8. At that point, I had to stop for a minute, dig my inhaler out of my kilt, and take a few puffs of albuterol. After another two minutes of walking, I got on my way again, feeling stronger, but nowhere near optimal. I knew I was going to finish this race ahead of my old record. More important, I was going to FINISH! I managed to stay hyrdated and avoid the medics, which, unfortunately, too many people needed as I passed them (including 2-3 on the ground being addressed, and two being driven away on gurneys).

Finally, I saw the Navy Yard sign. I was .25 miles from the end. This was it. Keep the mojo. Keep the legs moving, breathe strong. HEY, look! There’s Jeremy! “Yo!” Keep going…there’s Mama Canning cheering me on! I’ve got this…I’ve got this…and I’ve crossed the finish line. Now time for the banana I just ran 10 miles for !

I did it. I set a new PR for myself by 5:30. Not quite what I wanted, but a PR is a PR. I got my medal, my bag of food, my cheesesteak from the DNation tent, and an awesome bottle opener, courtesy of my co-captain.
